History and Natural Significance

Here's the thing about Yellow Hornets - they're relatively new arrivals to the UK. Originally from East Asia (think Japan, Korea, eastern China), they've only been confirmed as breeding residents here since around 2019. The first established populations were found in Hampshire, but they've been spreading steadily northwards. Finding one in Norfolk suggests they're doing pretty well at making themselves at home in British conditions.

Yellow Hornets are social wasps, living in colonies that can contain several hundred individuals at their peak. The colony cycle starts in spring when a mated queen emerges from hibernation and begins building a nest. She'll start with a small paper nest (made from chewed wood pulp mixed with saliva - lovely!) and lay her first batch of eggs.

The big question with any non-native species is what impact they'll have on local ecosystems. With Yellow Hornets, wel there is not a full answer yet. It is too soon to tell. On the positive side, they're efficient predators of various pest insects, and they don't seem to be as aggressive towards honeybees as some other hornet species.
