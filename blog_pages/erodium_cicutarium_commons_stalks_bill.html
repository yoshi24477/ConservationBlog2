History and Natural Significance

Common Stork's-bill (Erodium cicutarium) is a small annual plant with feathery, deeply-divided leaves and tiny pink or white flowers, commonly found growing in disturbed ground, path edges, and thin soils around campus. It gets its name from the distinctive long, pointed seed pods that resemble a stork's beak and spring open to scatter seeds when ripe.

The name "Stork's-bill" might sound a bit random, but it makes perfect sense once you see the seed pods. The fruits develop into these long, pointed structures that look exactly like a stork's beak - hence the name! The Latin Erodium actually comes from the Greek word for heron.

Clearly someone back in the day was really into the whole "bird beak" vibe when naming this plant family.

What really impressed me about this little plant was how perfectly adapted it is to the kind of disturbed habitats you find around university campuses. It's an annual, which means it goes from seed to flower to seed again in one growing season. This might seem like a risky strategy, but it's actually brilliant for colonising new areas quickly.

If you want to find Common Stork's-bill on campus, look in the kinds of places most people ignore - edges of paths, disturbed ground, areas where the grass is thin or patchy. The flowers are quite small (only about 1cm across) and can be pink or white, but the distinctive feathery leaves are often easier to spot than the flowers themselves.

Best time to look is probably March through September, though I've seen it flowering much later in mild autumns.
